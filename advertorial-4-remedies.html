The four remedies I tried — and exactly where each one falls short

Here's the honest scorecard. None of these are scams, exactly. They each do something. But every one of them misses the same piece, which I'll get to.

Compression socksThey squeeze the leg from the outside, which can help a little while you've got them on. But they don't make the muscles inside your leg do anything — and wrestling them on over swollen feet is a daily battle in itself. The moment they're off, you're back where you started.
Water pillsA neighbour swore by them. All they really did was send me back and forth to the bathroom. They move fluid out for a while, but they don't touch why the fluid is settling in your legs in the first place. My legs were just as heavy by evening.
Putting your feet upLovely for about ten minutes. Gravity does you a favour while your feet are raised — and then you stand up and the heaviness creeps straight back. It's a pause button, not a solution.
Massage guns & foot rollersThey feel pleasant on the surface and ease tightness for a bit. But they're working on the skin and soft tissue, not on the actual mechanism that moves blood up out of your legs. Same heaviness the next afternoon.

The one thing every single one of them misses

It took a friend who'd been a physiotherapist to point out the obvious thing I'd been missing for years. The blood in your legs has to travel uphill to get back to your heart, against gravity the whole way. Your heart does part of that — but a huge amount of it depends on your calf muscles. Every time they contract, they squeeze the veins and push blood upward, like a second pump tucked into each leg. Doctors call it the calf muscle pump.

That pump only works when the muscles move. As we get older and sit more, they fire far less often, the pump slows, and blood and fluid pool in the lower legs. That pooling is the heavy, swollen, tired feeling — and the cold, prickly feet at night.

Every remedy I'd tried worked on the symptom from the outside. Not one of them switched the pump itself back on. That was the missing piece the whole time.

Compression squeezes from outside the pump. Water pills bail out the result. Feet-up borrows from gravity. Massagers work the surface above it. The one thing none of them did was make the calf muscles actually contract — which is the only thing that drives the pump. So I went looking for something that did exactly that.
