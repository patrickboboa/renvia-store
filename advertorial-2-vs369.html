What I learned about how these devices actually work

Here's the thing nobody selling you the $369 unit goes out of their way to explain. The blood in your legs has to travel uphill to get back to your heart. Your heart does part of that work, but a huge amount of it depends on your calf muscles — every time they contract, they squeeze the veins and push blood upward, like a second pump tucked into each leg. As we get older and move less, those muscles fire less often, the pump slows down, and fluid settles in the lower legs. That pooling is the heavy, swollen, tired feeling.

The devices that help with this all use the same basic idea: EMS — Electrical Muscle Stimulation. Gentle, painless pulses make those calf muscles contract and relax on their own, "exercising" the pump for you while you sit still. That's it. That's the technology in the $369 device. And here's what surprised me: it's the same technology in units costing a fraction of that. The expensive brand wasn't selling me better science. It was selling me a famous name and a TV ad budget.

The technology was never the barrier. The price was. Once I understood that, paying triple felt like paying for the commercials, not the relief.
